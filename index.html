Recent Work

Excision project

Project summary:
• Branch excision is a commonly used method for measuring gas exchange, however, previous studies of this method have been narrow in scope. Here, we test excision methodology to determine the effects of excision on gas exchange and water stress based on a variety of species and plant traits.
• We collected paired measurements of gas exchange and water potential on intact and excised branches to determine the effect of excision. Measurements were performed on a variety of species that fit a range of drought strategies and xylem transport mechanisms.
• Excision relieved tension in the water column and caused a decrease in photosynthesis (A) and stomatal conductance (gs). We advise that the choice of using the excision method be informed by questions and goals of each unique study.
• We recommend that authors wishing to use the branch excision method first estimate the excision effects on their particular species and sites and develop their own correction factor for this method.

Climate gradient project

Project summary:
• Warming air temperatures are increasing plant mortality globally, yet plant photosynthetic responses to changing temperature are poorly understood. There are two main mechanisms thought to shape temperature response: the rates of enzyme reactions during photosynthesis and leaf to air vapor pressure deficit (VPD).
• We measure plant photosynthetic temperature response along two elevational climate gradients in South Africa and Norway to better understand how thermal traits are changing with shifts in climate.
• Optimal temperature, thermal breadth, and activation and deactivation energy remained constant across both climate gradients, suggesting the existence of an alternative mechanism responsible for plant success across a climate gradient, even when temperatures are greater or less than optimal. Recent studies have claimed to disentangle the effects of enzymatic reaction rates and VPD; however, we show that correlation between leaf temperature and vapor pressure deficit introduces multicollinearity, thus destabilizing the model and preventing disentanglement.
• Our results indicate that plants are well prepared for climate change as they already have strategies in place to achieve close to optimal temperatures and photosynthetic rate, regardless of surrounding air temperatures and conditions. Further work is required to experimentally disentangle the effects of VPD and enzyme reaction rates to correctly assess the relative importance.

Eddy covariance project

Project summary:
With the current pattern of warming, I seek to understand how plants will be able to adapt to new climates that include increased extremes. Based on recent literature, we hypothesize that plants can decouple their carbon assimilation and water loss in order to perform evaporative cooling, even when carbon gain is impossible. It has previously been our understanding that plants maximize carbon gain and minimize water loss. However, this understanding has been challenged by recent literature, and this study will provide one of the first fully natural studies of this phenomenon in the Pacific Northwest.

Electrical signalling project

Project summary:
Plants produce electrical signals which have traditionally been measured in voltage, but which have an unknown relation to plant health due to the unreliability of the voltage measurement. Using measurements of amperage, rather than voltage, we measured the electrical stress signatures produced by Douglas-fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii) trees in response to mechanical wounding. The electrical signature, which consists of a baseline signal, a spike initiated by wounding, and a subsequent signal recovery period, was measured along with controls for temperature, season, time-of-day, and soil type. We then compared the electrical stress signatures between healthy and unhealthy trees. We show that healthy trees have a characteristic electrical stress signature which is different from the electrical stress signature in unhealthy trees. Our results suggest that electrical stress signatures in amperage can be measured to reliably diagnose tree health providing a promising method for monitoring and early detection of changes in forest health.
